About me

Information About me

I am a PhD candidate at IIT Hyderabad, driven by a curiosity to understand the why and how behind aerosol–cloud–meteorology–precipitation interactions. My research explores how aerosols influence rainfall and extreme weather, combining atmospheric science with machine learning, Explainable AI, remote sensing, GIS, and data science to uncover meaningful insights. Previously, I worked at the North Eastern Space Application Centre (NESAC), developing hydrological models for flood early warning systems. I am passionate about translating complex Earth system processes into knowledge that supports better weather prediction, climate resilience, and informed policy.

11-Part Tutorial Series

Building a Research Computing Environment

A complete practical guide taking researchers from a blank machine to a fully configured, reproducible, and GPU-enabled open-science workstation.

Expand Series

Series Preface & Blueprint

An introductory blueprint outlining what you will learn throughout this 11-part guide to building a modern computational research environment.

Phase 1: The Local Workbench (Parts 1 – 4)

1. Installing WSL2, Ubuntu & Miniconda

Step-by-step installation of WSL2/Ubuntu on Windows 11, followed by unified Miniconda setup for both WSL2 and native Linux users.

2. Terminal Basics & Navigation

Command-line essentials for navigating the Linux filesystem, managing directories, and mastering absolute versus relative file paths.

3. Managing Projects with Conda

Isolating Python project dependencies using Miniconda to safely create, activate, and manage dedicated research environments.

4. Setting Up VS Code

Configuring Visual Studio Code as your primary editor and seamlessly connecting it to your WSL and Conda environments.

Phase 2: The Research Infrastructure (Parts 5 – 7)

5. Linux Essentials for HPC & Servers

A foundational map for remote servers covering directory permissions, system software management via apt, SSH, and SLURM scheduling.

6. Git & GitHub for Researchers

Configuring Git, setting up secure SSH authentication for GitHub, and mastering the daily four-command version control workflow.

7. Organizing Scientific Data

Implementing reusable directory templates, keeping raw data read-only, and explicitly separating codebases from large scientific datasets.

Phase 3: High-Performance Computing (Parts 8 – 9)

8. Enabling GPU Computing with CUDA

NVIDIA driver configuration for Windows/WSL and native Linux environments, explicitly designed to avoid system-level CUDA Toolkit conflicts.

9. PyTorch & TensorFlow Setup

Building isolated Conda environments with pip-bundled CUDA packages for deep learning frameworks, complete with GPU benchmarks.

Phase 4: Open Science (Parts 10 – 11)

10. Reproducible Research Workflows

Exporting environment dependencies, decoupling configurations via YAML/dotenv files, and packaging computational workflows with Docker.

11. Contributing to Open Science

Applying open-source licenses, minting citable Zenodo DOIs, executing pull requests, and building a public developer portfolio.

2026 ARM Big Open Data Science Summer School

What is the ARM Summer School about?
The ARM Summer School is a training program organized by the U.S. Department of Energy's Atmospheric Radiation Measurement (ARM) User Facility that equips students and early-career researchers with modern approaches to atmospheric science using observations, scientific computing, artificial intelligence, and open-science practices. This year's program was hosted at the National Weather Center (NWC), University of Oklahoma, in collaboration with the Cooperative Institute for Severe and High-Impact Weather Research and Operations (CIWRO).
What I learned
The program provided valuable insights into the observational infrastructure, computational workflows, and open-science practices that support atmospheric research. It was also a wonderful opportunity to exchange ideas with researchers from the ARM User Facility, Argonne National Laboratory, CIWRO, and the University of Oklahoma, while building new friendships with fellow participants who share a passion for atmospheric science, AI, and open science.

Dates: 18-22 May 2026

Location: National Weather Center, Oklahoma, USA | ARM Summer School 2026 Website | Hackathon Project

2026 ACM Second Asian School on High-Performance Computing and Artificial Intelligence

What is the ACM Asian School about?
The ACM Second Asian School on High-Performance Computing and Artificial Intelligence is a training program organized by the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) that equips students and early-career researchers with modern approaches to scientific computing, high-performance computing (HPC), artificial intelligence, and large-scale numerical simulations. This year's program was hosted at the RIKEN Center for Computational Science (R-CCS), Kobe, Japan, where participants received hands-on training using the Fugaku supercomputer and the SCALE-RM cloud-resolving model.
What I learned
The program provided valuable insights into high-performance computing workflows, large-scale numerical simulations, and AI-driven scientific computing. It was also a wonderful opportunity to interact with researchers, instructors, and fellow participants from across Asia while exchanging ideas and building new friendships with people who share a passion for computational science, Earth system science, and artificial intelligence.

Dates: 30 January - 3 February 2026

Location: RIKEN Center for Computational Science, Kobe, Japan | 2026 ACM Asian School on HPC and AI Website
